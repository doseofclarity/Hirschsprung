Hirschsprung Disease

"Congenital absence of ganglion cells in the distal colon → functional obstruction." – Pillitteri

Hirschsprung Disease Like You Are 5 Years Old

🧸 Imagine the large intestine (colon) is like a long squishy tube that pushes poop out.

• In Hirschsprung disease, a small piece at the end (near the anus) is missing special nerves called ganglion cells.
• Without those nerves, that piece stays tight and squeezed all the time – like a clogged pipe.
• Poop builds up behind the tight part, and the colon above gets very big (megacolon).

🩺 Doctors can remove the tight part and sew the healthy colon back so poop can go out easily.

Aganglionic segment (no nerves) → narrow → proximal dilation

Definition & Epidemiology

Hirschsprung disease (HD) – congenital absence of ganglion cells in the myenteric (Auerbach) and submucosal (Meissner) plexuses, usually in the distal colon, leading to functional obstruction. Also called congenital aganglionic megacolon.

📊 Incidence
1 in 5,000 live births. More common in males (M:F ratio 4:1). Most cases are sporadic, but familial forms exist.
🇵🇭 Philippines
Estimated 100‑150 new cases annually. Late diagnosis still occurs due to misdiagnosis as chronic constipation. Most common in full‑term infants (rare in preterm).
⚠️ Key point: The aganglionic segment always begins at the internal anal sphincter and extends proximally for a variable length. Short‑segment disease (rectosigmoid) occurs in 80% of cases.

Pathophysiology & Genetics

🔬 Pathophysiology:
Lack of ganglion cells → absence of inhibitory (nitrinergic) neurons → failure of relaxation of the affected segment → functional obstruction → proximal bowel dilation (megacolon) → enterocolitis (bacterial overgrowth) → perforation risk.
🧬 Genetics:
• RET proto‑oncogene mutations account for 50% of familial and 15‑20% of sporadic cases.
• Associated with Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome) – 2‑10% of HD patients have Down syndrome.
• Other genes: EDNRB, EDN3, SOX10.
📌 Associated syndromes: Down syndrome, Waardenburg syndrome, MEN2 (due to RET mutations), Mowat‑Wilson syndrome.

Clinical Features

👶 Newborn (first 24‑48 hours):
• Failure to pass meconium (delayed >48 hours) – cardinal sign
• Abdominal distension, bilious vomiting
• Refusal to feed, irritability
• Rectal examination may explode with gush of stool and gas (after withdrawal of finger)
🧒 Infant / Toddler (short‑segment):
• Chronic constipation since birth
• Ribbon‑like stools, failure to thrive
• Distended abdomen, palpable fecal mass
• Enterocolitis: explosive diarrhea, fever, septic shock (life‑threatening)
🚨 Hirschsprung‑associated enterocolitis (HAEC): Sudden fever, vomiting, explosive foul‑smelling diarrhea, abdominal distension → medical emergency.

Diagnosis & Tests

  • Contrast enema (barium enema): Shows transitional zone (narrow aganglionic segment → dilated proximal colon).
  • Anorectal manometry: Absence of rectoanal inhibitory reflex (RAIR) – diagnostic in older children (not reliable in newborns).
  • Rectal suction biopsy (gold standard): Histology shows absence of ganglion cells and presence of hypertrophic nerve trunks.
  • Calretinin immunohistochemistry: Absent staining in aganglionic segment.
  • Genetic testing: RET mutation analysis for familial cases and syndromic presentations.
🔍 Gold standard: Rectal suction biopsy (absence of ganglion cells).

Management & Surgical Procedures

💊 Pre‑operative (decompression):
• IV fluids, NPO, nasogastric decompression
• Rectal irrigations (normal saline) daily to prevent enterocolitis
• Treat enterocolitis with IV antibiotics (metronidazole + gentamicin), fluids
⚕️ Definitive surgical procedures (pull‑through):
• Swenson (resect aganglionic segment, anastomosis)
• Soave (endorectal pull‑through)
• Duhamel (retrorectal anastomosis)
• Temporary colostomy may be needed in critically ill infants (if enterocolitis or massive distension)
📌 Surgical principles: Resect the entire aganglionic segment and pull through normal ganglionic colon to the anus. Done at 1‑3 months of age (after diagnosis, before severe enterocolitis).

Nursing Care & Complications

👩‍⚕️ Pre‑operative nursing:
• Monitor abdominal girth, stool output
• Perform saline irrigations (prescribed) – warm, low pressure, using a soft catheter
• Strict I&O, daily weight
• Educate parents about signs of enterocolitis
🍼 Post‑operative care (after pull‑through):
• Assess for anastomotic leak (fever, peritonitis)
• Rectal dilations (if prescribed to prevent stricture)
• Monitor bowel function (diarrhea common initially)
• Skin care (perineal irritation from frequent stools)
• Pain management, early feeding as tolerated
⚠️ Complications: • Enterocolitis (most common, can be fatal)
• Anastomotic leak, stricture
• Incontinence (transient after pull‑through)
• Constipation (residual aganglionosis or transition zone pull‑through)
• Perianal excoriation

Prevention & Family Education

  • Prevention: No known primary prevention; prenatal detection of megacolon is possible by ultrasound (third trimester). Genetic counselling for families with RET mutations.
  • Parent teaching (pre‑op): How to perform rectal irrigations, signs of enterocolitis (fever, explosive diarrhea, abdominal distension), when to seek emergency care.
  • Parent teaching (post‑op): Rectal dilation schedule, perineal care, expected bowel patterns (frequent loose stools initially), follow‑up for fecal continence.
  • Long‑term follow‑up: Monitor for constipation, soiling, growth; most children achieve normal bowel control by school age.
📞 Support resources: Philippine Society of Pediatric Gastroenterology, parent support groups for Hirschsprung disease.
